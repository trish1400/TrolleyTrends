What's the purpose for collecting this data and what will be done with it?

Your Trolley Trends data could be very valuable in observing the Cost of Living crisis, it could be used to identify rising prices or shrink-flation. It could be used to enhance or interface with other datasets to understand calorific consumption trends or you could see how your shopping habits compare to others in the same area. Exactly how it will be used has not been defined upfront, this is permitted as the data is anonymous.

The intention is to make the dataset available to anyone who wants to use it under an Open Data Commons licence.

However, before any public release, it will be necessary to further evaluate the likelihood of identifiability and this is likely to depend on the size and variance of the data collected.

What data is collected?

Three datasets are submitted. The datasets and a description of the fields are:

  • Purchases data
    • guid - a theoretically unique identifier generated based on the contents of this record (one-way hash), this is so that if the record is resubmitted, the duplicate can be identified
    • date - the date, with the time removed, on which the purchase was made
    • storeName - the name of the store as supplied in the original data
    • storeId - the storeId as supplied in the original data
    • storeFormat - the storeFormat as supplied in the original data
    • purchaseType - the purchaseType as supplied in the original data
    • basketValueGross - the basketValueGross shuffled within 20p of the original value
    • basketValueNet - the basketValueNet shuffled by the same amount as basketValueGross
    • overallBasketSavings - the overallBasketSavings as supplied in the original data
    • totalItems - the total number of items as counted from the original data
  • Products data
    • date - the date, with the time removed, on which the purchase was made
    • name - the name of the product as supplied in the original data
    • price - the price of the product as supplied in the original data
    • storeId - the storeId as supplied in the original data
    • storeName - the name of the store as supplied in the original data
    • storeFormat - the storeFormat as supplied in the original data
    • guid - a theoretically unique identifier generated based on the contents of this record (one-way hash), this is so that if the record is resubmitted, the duplicate can be identified
  • Weekly data
    • weekCommencing - the date of the Monday of the week which this data is summed for
    • totalBasketValueGross - the sum of basketValueGross for purchases made within this week shuffled within 20p of the original value
    • totalBasketValueNet - the sum of basketValueNet for purchases made within this week shuffled by the same amount as the totalBasketValueGross
    • totalOverallBasketSavings - the sum of overallBasketSavings for purchases made within this week
    • totalItems - the total number of items for all purchases made within this week
    • submission - a theoretically unique randomly generated identifier for this submission. This enables weeks in the same submission to be grouped, but a subsequent submission containing the same records would generate a new identifier
    • outcode - the first part of the postcode (to the left of the space) taken from the Clubcard account postal address field. This will indicate the current region of residence.

The three datasets do not have keys which relate them to each other.

The Weekly data dataset is the only dataset where the records are grouped by submission.

How is the data anonymous and not merely pseudo-anonymous?

Psuedo-anonymous data does not have any individual identifiers but can, with the use of additional information, be attributed back to an individual. Anonymous data cannot be related back to an individual by 'means reasonably likely to be used'.

Pseudo-anonymised data is still considered personal data and so falls under the remit of GDPR and other data production legislation.

To collect, retain and share this data, it is important to ensure it is anonymous.

N.B. The Clubcard data may refer to multiple individuals within a single household, or with another relationship, however, as it may also relate to a single person, 'individual' is the term that has been used and applied.

Test cases for anonymisation vs psuedo-anonymisation

The below contains the challenges and reasoning to support that the data is pseudo-anonymised.

The following supplementary personal information is considered to be 'reasonably likely to be used':

  • Observation of an individual checkout (store, time, potentially the amount spent, and potentially some items purchased).
  • A receipt belonging to a known individual (store, time, amount spent, and items purchased).
  • A bank or credit card statement (store, date, and amount spent).
  • An awareness of the postcode outcode (the part to the left of the space) of the individual's residence.
  • A version of the JSON data that was submitted (but not containing all the same information).

Would it be possible to identify exactly what products the individual purchased?

No.

  • There is no relationship between a purchase and the items purchased or between products purchased together, so it would not be possible to retrieve the items relating to that purchase.

Would it be possible to retrieve additional information about a purchase?

Reasonably unlikely.

  • Assuming that the store, purchase time and basketValueNet (amount spent) are known - the additional information that could be available are the totalItems, basketValueGross and overallBasketSavings.
  • Only the date (not the time) is collected for a purchase, this significantly reduces the likelihood of identifying a single purchase in the data as many purchases will be made in a store on a single day.
  • The amount spent (basketValueNet) is offset by up to 20p, this data perturbation makes it further unlikely that anyone could reasonably identify the purchase record in order to retrieve the additional (very minimal) data.

Would it be possible to identify what other purchases the individual made?

No.

  • There is no relationship between a purchase and other purchases made by the same individual.

Would it be possible to identify how much the individual spent overall or in other weeks?

Reasonably unlikely.

  • The total purchases made within a week are summed (data generalisation) without the store information but with the postcode outcode.
  • This breaks a potentially known interaction with a single store and should introduce a wider subset of potential data subjects.
  • As the amounts are summed, it would be necessary to know all the purchases for a whole week and not just a single purchase event.
  • The amounts spent are offset by up to 20p, this data perturbation means that, even if only one purchase was made in a week, it is unlikely someone could reasonably, and with confidence, identify the weekly records made by an individual

If I submitted a subsequent (or earlier) version of the JSON data for the same Clubcard, would that give me a key to identify information from a previous submission?

No.

  • Any identifiers that are generated (hashed) from the data itself are for single records - so a product or a purchase. This makes it possible to identify that the data submitted is a duplicate, but there is no relationship between those records and others.
  • For the weekly data, which does retain a relationship between records, the identifier is randomly generated per submission, so if the data was submitted again another identifier would be generated and it would not be possible to relate these records to the previously submitted records.
